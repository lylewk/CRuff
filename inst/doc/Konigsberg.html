We will first do the default call to “K_run” which uses the femur equation from Konigsberg and Meadows Jantz (2018) and applies it to the “European” data set from Ruff (2018). See “European data set: Excel” and “Notes” under “Special Projects” here. Now for a quick word about Konigsberg and Meadows Jantz (2018). There are not actually any such equations in Konigsberg and Meadows Jantz (2018). Instead, the classical calibration method given in Konigsberg et al. (1998) is used to calculate equations from the summary multivariate statistics in Konigsberg and Meadows Jantz (2018).

Let’s see for the total data set whether the proportion of cases in the 50 percent prediction interval actually contains 50 percent of the data. That is a straight-forward binomial test:

sto=156+636+270
print(sto)
#> [1] 1062
print(binom.test(636,sto,0.5))
#> 
#>  Exact binomial test
#> 
#> data:  636 and sto
#> number of successes = 636, number of trials = 1062, p-value = 1.244e-10
#> alternative hypothesis: true probability of success is not equal to 0.5
#> 95 percent confidence interval:
#>  0.5686809 0.6285098
#> sample estimates:
#> probability of success 
#>              0.5988701

OK, not good. The probability value is quite low, because the 95% confidence interval for the proportion in the 50% prediction interval is from about 0.5687 to 0.6285. It is even worse if we compare the proportion of cases below the 50% PIs to the total of those above and those below.

sto=156+270
print(sto)
#> [1] 426
print(binom.test(156,sto,0.5))
#> 
#>  Exact binomial test
#> 
#> data:  156 and sto
#> number of successes = 156, number of trials = 426, p-value = 3.656e-08
#> alternative hypothesis: true probability of success is not equal to 0.5
#> 95 percent confidence interval:
#>  0.3203436 0.4139235
#> sample estimates:
#> probability of success 
#>              0.3661972

OK, so that shows that the bulk of the cases (actual statures) are below the 50% PIs. We would have expected about half should be below and half should be above. But, that is not happening because the estimated statures are biased. Generally, the estimates are greater than the actual statures, as in the plot.

Awful! The Konigsberg and Meadows Jantz (2018) equation is clearly over-estimating stature for the tall (while underestimating stature for the short). This is the reverse of what we expect for “inverse” calibration (regressing stature on long bone length). So, apparently the classical calibration has over-corrected. Let’s see how Sjøvold (1990) does as that (in theory) will not over-correct.
